What is Capture The Flag

Learning security as a game

Capture The Flag, or CTF, is a security competition where you solve puzzles to find hidden strings called flags, written in a format such as flag{...}. Jeopardy-style events offer a board of standalone challenges across categories like cryptography, web, forensics, and reverse engineering, and are ideal for beginners. Attack-defense events have teams defend their own running services while attacking everyone else, which mirrors real operations. CTFs are safe and legal because everything happens in a controlled environment built for the game, and they are how both students and professionals sharpen real skills.

TA note: Show students picoctf.org picoGym right now. Let them browse the challenge list. The categories (General Skills, Cryptography, Web, Forensics, Reverse Engineering) map to exactly what they learned this week. A student who can solve one Crypto challenge has real skills.

Red team and blue team

Attack, defend, and improve

Security exercises split into roles. The red team are authorized attackers who simulate real adversaries to find weaknesses before criminals do. The blue team are the defenders who monitor systems, detect intrusions, and respond to contain them. A purple team is the two working together so that every attack teaches the defenders something. Understanding both sides makes you better at either one, and the constant rule is permission: red teams operate only with written authorization, which is the legal and ethical line that separates a professional from a criminal.

TA note: The key legal point is permission. A red team has written authorization. The same action without authorization is a crime under the Computer Fraud and Abuse Act. This is not a technicality. Students should hear this explicitly.

What is a security incident?

Recognizing when something is wrong

A security incident is any event that threatens the confidentiality, integrity, or availability of a system or its data. Not every alert is an incident (false positives exist), and not every incident is a breach (some are caught in time). Common types include unauthorized access, malware infection, data exfiltration, denial of service, and insider threats. Organizations categorize incidents by severity -- from low (a single failed login) to critical (ransomware encrypting all files). The first job of a blue-team analyst is to separate real incidents from noise.

TA note: Ask: if you saw 500 failed SSH logins to your server from an IP in another country, is that an incident? Probably yes. If your mom typed her password wrong twice, is that an incident? Probably no. Judgment is the skill.

Log formats and what to look for

Reading the timeline of a system

A log is a timestamped record of an event on a system. Common log types include authentication logs (who logged in or tried to), access logs (which files or pages were requested), system logs (OS events), and network logs (connections made). Logs use structured formats so they can be searched. A typical authentication log line looks like: 2026-06-22 09:06:14 FAIL user=admin src=192.168.0.47. The fields are always: when, what happened, who, and from where. Analysts look for anomalies: events that are unusual in timing, frequency, source, or account.

TA note: Show the Python log list from the notebook. Walk through it line by line: time, username, result. Ask: which line is suspicious? The string of FAILs then a SUCCESS is the classic brute-force pattern.

Reading logs

Finding the story in the events

Logs are timestamped records of what happened on a system, from logins to network connections, and they are the defender main source of evidence. An analyst looks for indicators that stand out from normal activity. A burst of many failed logins for one account followed by a success, for example, is the classic signature of a brute-force or password-guessing attack. Security teams centralize logs in a SIEM (Security Information and Event Management) tool so they can search and correlate events across many machines, turning scattered records into a clear timeline of an incident.

TA note: Show the log triage pattern live in the notebook: many fails then a success on the same account = brute force. Ask: what would you do if you saw this on a real system? Answers: lock the account, alert the user, investigate the IP. These are real SOC responses.

Decoding flags

Putting the week together

CTF crypto and forensics challenges reward the exact skills you built this week. A flag might be Base64-encoded, shifted with a Caesar cipher, combined with XOR, or hidden in file metadata, and many combine steps so you must reverse them in order. The approach is to recognize the encoding or cipher from its shape, then apply the right tool, whether that is a quick Python snippet, CyberChef, or dcode.fr. Working multi-step flags teaches patience and pattern recognition, the same habits used in real forensic analysis.

CTF tip: check for Base64 first (strings ending in = or ==). Also watch for hex (only 0-9 and A-F), ROT13 (all letters, nearly readable), and URL encoding (%41 is the letter A). Identifying the encoding is half the solve.

Incident response

A calm plan under pressure

When something goes wrong, defenders follow a repeatable plan so they do not panic. A common model has six phases: prepare in advance, detect and analyze the event, contain it to stop the spread, eradicate the attacker foothold, recover systems to normal, and capture lessons learned afterward. Speed and clear communication matter as much as technical skill, because a fast, contained response limits the damage. Writing a blameless review afterward turns one incident into lasting improvement for the whole team.

Cybersecurity careers

Where these skills lead

Cybersecurity is a large field with a persistent shortage of skilled people, so the skills from this week open real doors. A SOC analyst watches for and responds to attacks, a penetration tester legally attacks systems to find weaknesses first, an incident responder cleans up after a breach, and a security engineer builds defenses into software and networks. Paths in often start with curiosity, school clubs, and competitions like picoCTF and CyberPatriot, followed later by certifications such as CompTIA Security+ and the Certified Ethical Hacker. The constant across every role is ethics: these skills are used with permission, to protect people.

TA note: Ask students to name a cybersecurity job title they have heard of. Then map it to the categories: SOC analyst (blue), penetration tester (red), malware analyst (forensics), security engineer (build). Point to picoCTF and CyberPatriot as the path from today to a career.
