Breach Case Studies

Cyber Squad 2026 - Day 1 Lab: Spot the Vulnerability. Each team takes one card and answers the four questions.

For your case, identify: (a) the ASSET, (b) the VULNERABILITY, (c) the THREAT that exploited it, (d) which CIA pillar broke, and (e) one CONTROL that could have prevented or limited it. Present a 2-minute summary.

1. Equifax (2017)

Equifax, a major US credit bureau, exposed the personal data of about 147 million people: names, Social Security numbers, birth dates, and addresses. Attackers got in through a known flaw in the Apache Struts web software. A patch had been available for about two months but was not applied. Attackers stayed in the network for roughly 76 days.

2. Yahoo (2013-2014, disclosed 2016)

Yahoo suffered the largest breach by user count in history: all 3 billion user accounts were affected. Stolen data included names, email addresses, phone numbers, security questions, and password hashes (some using the weak MD5 algorithm). The breach was disclosed years later, during Yahoo's sale to Verizon, which reduced the sale price.

3. Target (2013)

Attackers stole about 40 million credit and debit card numbers (and personal data on up to 70 million people) during the holiday season. They first got in using network credentials stolen from a third-party HVAC vendor, then installed malware on the in-store checkout (point-of-sale) systems to skim card data.

4. SolarWinds (2020)

Attackers (a nation-state group) secretly inserted malicious code into a software update for SolarWinds Orion, a network-management tool. About 18,000 organizations downloaded the poisoned update, including US government agencies and large companies. This is called a supply-chain attack - compromise the trusted vendor to reach everyone downstream.

Recurring pattern: unpatched systems, stolen or over-trusted credentials, weak crypto, and trusted third parties. The threat in each case was a skilled attacker; the missing controls were ordinary, affordable best practices.
