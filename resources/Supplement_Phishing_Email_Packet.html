Phishing Email Packet

Cyber Squad 2026 - Day 2 Lab: Phishing Detective. Bowie State University.

Instructions: Read each of the 10 emails below. For each one, decide if it is SAFE (legitimate) or PHISHING, and write down the specific red flags you spot. The red flags to look for: fake or mismatched sender, generic greeting, urgency or threats, bad grammar, suspicious links, unexpected attachments, and requests for passwords or codes. The answer key is on the last page.

The Inbox

E1From: Amazon <billing@amaz0n-security.shop>   Subject: URGENT: Your account is on hold
Dear Customer, We detect unusual activity. You're account will be CLOSED within 24 hours unless you verify you're identity. Click here to verify now. Failure to act will result in permanent suspension.
E2From: Ms. Rivera <arivera@bowiestate.edu>   Subject: Cyber Squad - bring a water bottle tomorrow
Hi everyone, great first day! Quick reminder that the building gets warm, so bring a water bottle tomorrow. We start at 9:00 with the news warm-up. See you then. - Ms. Rivera
E3From: IT Help Desk <support@micros0ft-team.com>   Subject: Password expires today
Your email password expires in 2 hours. To keep your account active, reply to this email with your current username and password so we can reset it for you.
E4From: Netflix <info@mailer.netflix.com>   Subject: New sign-in to your account
Hi Jordan, we noticed a new sign-in on a Smart TV in Bowie, MD. If this was you, no action is needed. If not, you can secure your account from the Netflix app or netflix.com. We never ask for your password by email.
E5From: USPS Delivery <tracking@usps-redelivery.info>   Subject: Your package could not be delivered
We attempted delivery but failed. A $1.99 fee is required to reschedule. Enter your card details here: usps-redelivery.info/pay. Act within 12 hours or your package returns to sender.
E6From: Google <no-reply@accounts.google.com>   Subject: Security alert
A new device signed in to your Google Account. If this was you, you can ignore this email. If not, review your activity at myaccount.google.com. We will never ask you to share your password or verification codes.
E7From: Principal Dawson <pdawson@scholarship-grants-now.biz>   Subject: You won a $5,000 scholarship!!!
Congratulationss!! You have been selected for a exclusive scholarship. To claim, send your full name, date of birth, school ID number, and a $25 processing fee via gift card. Reply ASAP, spots are limited!!!
E8From: GitHub <noreply@github.com>   Subject: Your sign-in code
Your one-time code is 481920. Enter it in the browser tab where you started signing in. If you did not try to sign in, change your password. GitHub will never ask you to share this code with anyone.
E9From: Bank of America <alerts@secure-bofa-verify.com>   Subject: Account locked - verify immediately
Dear valued customer, your account has been temporarily locked. To restore access, confirm your card number, PIN, and the code we just texted you at the link below. This is your final notice.
E10From: Coach Lee <clee@bowiestate.edu>   Subject: Soccer practice moved to 4pm
Team, practice tomorrow moves from 3:00 to 4:00 because the field is in use. Same place. Text me if you cannot make it. - Coach Lee

Answer Key (for instructors)

E1 PHISHING - fake sender domain (amaz0n-security.shop), generic greeting, urgency/threat, bad grammar ("you're identity"), suspicious link.

E2 SAFE - real school domain, personal and specific, no links or requests for info.

E3 PHISHING - look-alike domain (micros0ft-team.com), urgency, and the giant red flag: asks you to reply with your password. No IT team ever does this.

E4 SAFE - legitimate Netflix domain, personalized, informational, explicitly says it never asks for your password.

E5 PHISHING - fake domain, small-fee scam to capture card details, urgency. Classic smishing/phishing pattern.

E6 SAFE - real google.com domain, no link to click for credentials, tells you to go to the official site yourself.

E7 PHISHING - non-school domain, too-good-to-be-true reward, bad grammar, asks for personal data and a gift-card fee. Gift cards are a scam hallmark.

E8 SAFE - real github.com domain. It is a normal MFA code email and warns you not to share the code. (Teaching point: the danger is if someone then calls asking you to read the code aloud.)

E9 PHISHING - fake domain, asks for card number, PIN, and your one-time code, urgency and "final notice." Banks never ask for your PIN or codes.

E10 SAFE - real school domain, specific and ordinary, no links or data requests.

Score: 5 phishing (E1, E3, E5, E7, E9) and 5 safe (E2, E4, E6, E8, E10). Discuss which fakes were most convincing and why.
