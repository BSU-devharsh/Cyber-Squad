# Cyber Squad - Day 5: Capstone Capture The Flag and Blue-Team Defense

**Platform:** U.S. Cyber Range Exercise Area (team CTF environment plus a defense exercise).
**Duration:** about 4 hours including the showcase.

---

## Learning objectives
- Combine the week's skills to solve a multi-category Capture The Flag.
- Experience the defender (blue team) side: spot and stop an attack.
- Reflect on cybersecurity careers and ethics.

## Vocabulary (acronyms expanded and defined)
- **CTF = Capture The Flag**: solve security puzzles to find hidden flags.
- **Red team / Blue team**: attackers / defenders in a security exercise.
- **SOC = Security Operations Center**: the team that monitors and defends an organization.
- **IDS = Intrusion Detection System**: software that watches for attacks.
- **Log**: a recorded list of events on a system.
- **OSINT = Open-Source Intelligence**: investigation using only public information.

## Instructor setup
1. In the U.S. Cyber Range, launch a CTF or capstone exercise with challenges spanning Linux, cryptography, and web (the skills from Days 1 to 4).
2. Optionally launch a defense scenario where students review logs to find signs of an attack.
3. Prepare a simple scoreboard and group students into squads.

## Student lab
1. **Capture The Flag (teams):** work through challenges in categories you have practiced. Use the terminal, your crypto skills, and your web knowledge. Record each flag.
2. **Blue-team round:** open the provided logs in the environment and answer: what time did the suspicious activity start, which account was involved, and what would you do to stop it?
3. **Showcase:** each squad presents one challenge they solved and one defense idea.

## Mini-lesson: the two sides
```
  RED  (attack): find the weakness     BLUE (defend): watch logs, patch, block
  Great security teams understand both. Ethics: always with permission.
```

## In the news (real and verifiable)
Capture The Flag competitions are a core part of major security conferences such as DEF CON, and free programs like picoCTF from Carnegie Mellon University have introduced very large numbers of students to the field. Demand for cybersecurity workers continues to outpace supply, which is why hands-on programs like this one exist.

## Careers and next steps
- Explore roles: SOC analyst, penetration tester, incident responder, security engineer.
- Keep practicing: school cyber clubs, the CyberPatriot program, and ongoing U.S. Cyber Range courses.
- Ethics reminder: skills learned here are used only with permission, to protect people and systems.

## Assessment
- Team score from the CTF.
- Blue-team answers (time, account, response action).
- Reflection: which day built the skill you are most proud of, and which career sounds most interesting?
